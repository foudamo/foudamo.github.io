Scrubbing CO2 Using
High Altitude Balloon

Engineering & Society Capstone.

  • Date: Sep 15 - Jan 16
  • Team Size: 5
  • Category: Engineering

It is well known that climate change is a growing global concern. So our challenge was to look at the possibility of reducing pollutants and greenhouse gases at a high altitude (~25 km), in order to combat climate change.

Why specifically at ~25 km high altitude? Because the challenge was to see if we can use VanWyn Inc’s technology (which was a high altitude balloon) to do so. VanWyn Inc is a start-up by McMaster’s alumni Erinn van Wynsberghe, in which they are looking at using a wirelessly-charged high-altitude balloon that can provide a cost-effective alternative to launching telecommunications satellite systems. So, why not use it to also compact climate change? That’s what we needed to figure out if possible or not.

This was be done via literature review of existing gas capturing and disposal methods. But first, we needed to find out who are the stakeholders that this project would concern. For any project to succeed, you need to make sure your project doesn’t make any of the stakeholders involved worse off, especially those who have high influence on it. That doesn’t mean you ignore the others (for example, “wildlife” who ranked low on influence on our chart), but it could mean making sure prioritizing “FCC” and “Canadian Army” approval first. Remember, all stakeholders must always be winners (or at least not worse off).

Next, we needed to determine what were the greenhouse gases we should be after. We found that carbon dioxide is by far the most concerning greenhouse gas presently. So that is the villain we are after in our story.

This is where all the fun happened. We brainstormed all sorts of crazy ideas. Some of which were labelled "James Bond Evil" by my teammates (that's the one in the bottom right corner, and it was my idea!). All in all, it was the best part of the project because it all got very technical after this.

From this point on, what the team was able to produce was a report with literature review of existing gas capturing and disposal methods as mentioned previously. We found that there are mainly 3 carbon capture and storage (CCS) technologies that exist currently, out of which some other methods are also derived from (the 3 stated in the picture above). Right off the bat, we found that the first technology isn’t suitable for our application, so we crossed it out. The other two technologies were described and compared in terms of cost, mass, energy, and associated resonance time of the high altitude balloon in the final report below.

Interestingly enough, most of the current CCS technologies are still in the research and development (R&D) phases. On the other hand, some of the existing ones were very upscale in size and didn’t translate very well into our project; and some were used in applications like submarines and space stations, which we couldn’t find a lot of information on. We also found that planting trees is not an efficient method for carbon capture for numerous reasons (they tend to release most of the carbon they absorb over the long run, so that doesn’t help).

Our conclusion was that we recommended that this application isn’t doable with this specific application at the moment because of the phase the current CCS technologies are at. We also recommended that at this stage, the next step is to either start R&D by either putting a team together to focus on solving this problem, or partner up with some of the teams already researching in this field.

Universal Software Remote Control

Human Computer Interaction Course.

  • Date: Sep 15 - Jan 16
  • Team Size: 4
  • Category: UX/UI

For this project, we were challenged to design a universal remote application for smartphones. The idea was how would we simplify the user experience around the multiple remote controllers needed to control the many multimedia gadgets found in a home today.

objective

The objective was to use design thinking principles, like the ones learnt from Don Norman's "Design of Everyday Things", in order to combine all of those remote controllers into one universal one, using the gadget everyone has on them all the time; smartphones.

research

We started by researching, who were we designing for? We came up with the following 4 users that we believed would be using a multimedia gadget in their home at some point.

Those 4 users were then given names and pictures in order to 'humanize' them. We used stock pictures that fit the character descriptions, or sometimes even famous characters from shows (like Frank Costanza from the Seinfeld show).

From that point on, everytime we ran into a problem we would look at those four and ask our themselves, 'how would Tanya use this?' or 'what would John think of this?', and so on.

Hackitmac Student Club

Branding & Graphic Design

  • Date: Sep 15 - Jan 16
  • Team Size: 1
  • Category: Branding & Graphic Design

Branding is one of the most essential things to any organization. It’s your image. How do you want the stakeholders you deal with to view you? How do you want people who don’t know you to have as a first impression?

This is how Hackitmac used to look:

And so a couple of notes here; for just a logo for our first year, this was more than fine. However, it wasn’t enough for our plans and dreams of where we wanted to take the club. We needed a cleaner logo that’s easier to read. We needed a full identity that screams our dreams and values and what we do as a club. We needed a full brand. So we created one. This is how Hackitmac looks now:

It’s a lot cleaner, and a lot higher resolution. But that’s not the point! The new branding represents us in a way that allows people’s first impression to have a visual of what we do as a community and as a club. Here’s how: The logo is a lot cleaner, and easier to read. We emphasized a lot on the “Hack” by separating the words by colour to allow for an easier faster read. We made sure to visualize what “hacking” & “hackathons” are all about by adding a pattern of moments and things that everyone who has ever been to a hackathon would relate to. A lot of people associate “hacking” with the illegal activities of stealing and cracking into people’s computers. But that’s not what we do! We are a community interested in learning, building, and experiencing with different cool technologies in order to solve real problems. We are diverse. We are positive. We love free swag. All those are things you will find in our pattern. Our main colours are different levels of Blue and different levels of orange/gold. Why? Typically colours are associated with meaning psychologically (seriously! This is so cool Google it up). Here’s how it goes; - Blue is associated with confidence, comfortable, and quiet; where - Orange is typically associated with creativity, innovation, and positive encouraging energy. This sums up our club and community precisely. We take risks by adding to our workloads and spending time travelling and building things; yet we are very confident in what we’re doing. We love diversity and working with others (in fact, a huge number of the club are not engineers and never coded before!) yet we’re very comfortable going outside of our comfort zones to be truly innovative. Lastly, we’re a very welcoming community that is self-motivated and encouraging towards each other, and is never too noisy for a voice to be heard :)
